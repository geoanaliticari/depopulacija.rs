The indicator Land Cover as a ‚ÄòTool for Monitoring‚Äô Population Change shows the transformation of cultivated land towards transitional categories of grass and forest ecosystems in the period 1990‚Äì2018, which is directly related to a low-intensity population activity or its complete absence in the period 1990‚Äì2015. The following datasets were used to calculate this indicator: Corine Land Cover (CLC) 1990, Version 2020_20u1 [European Environmental Agency, Copernicus Land Monitoring Service, 2020], Corine Land Cover (CLC) 2018, Version 2020_20u1 [European Environmental Agency, Copernicus Land Monitoring Service, 2020], GHS-POP R2019A - GHS population grid multitemporal [European Commission, Joint Research Center, 2019], i GeoSrbija [Open data of the National Data Infrastructure, Republic Geodetic Authority, n.d.].

The conversion of arable land, where human activity is visible, takes place gradually through several phases. The first phase is expressed through the transformation of agricultural land towards areas with complex cultivation patterns and the presence of natural vegetation, with a tendency to extensify production. This type of land is usually used to meet the essential needs of the local population. The most intensive reduction of this land cover category is observed in Vojvodina, Pomoravlje, the Timok region and the Peri-Pannonian realm, which are highly intensive agricultural regions. At the same time, these zones are characterised by a population increase. On the other hand, southwestern and western Serbia, as well as parts of southern and eastern Serbia are marked by a significant increase in areas with a complex cultivation pattern, which can be explained by: (1) the diversification of activities, especially in the industrial area (Majdanpek, U≈æice, Krupanj, Prokuplje, Kladovo, etc.) where agriculture is an ancillary activity; (2) specialisation in the greenhouse-type or fruit production in the peri-urban zone of larger centres; (3) sub-essential agricultural production and a gradual abandonment of agriculture in the mountainous part of central Serbia, which indicates a reduced population activity.

As far as grassland in the territory of Serbia are concerned, the most intensive transformations are reflected in the mountainous and border areas of East Serbia, Banat and on the plateaus of southwestern Serbia. In these areas, land conversion is directed towards reducing areas under pastures and increasing the share of cultivated grassland. This indirectly indicates an increased agricultural activity and positive population trends.

The last phase of the land cover transformation is marked by a rising share of woodland scrub and forest areas. It indirectly illustrates the abandonment of space, i.e. the reduced intensity of activities and the absence of population. In the mountainous area of East Serbia, changes have taken place in favour of forest land (especially in the Homolje region).
